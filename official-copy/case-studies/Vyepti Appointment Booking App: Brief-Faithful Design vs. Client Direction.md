## Context

Vyepti (eptinezumab-jjmr) is a pharmaceutical brand by Lundbeck — an IV migraine prevention medication for adults. Their appointment booking app was commissioned to solve a real operational problem: nurses and secretaries were managing IV treatment schedules manually, relying on paper forms and paper patient records, with no standardized process for assigning treatment programs to patients.

The brief, as submitted in Vyepti's onboarding form, outlined 7 functional flows designed to automate bookings, digitize patient records, and provide staff with clear SOPs. As the sole UX/UI Designer at Webpuppies — a small Singaporean IT agency — the project was designed from that brief, working through Ian (PM) as the primary communication channel. Direct access to the end users — nurses and secretaries — was not part of the engagement.

---

## Impact

The project produced two complete design directions under a single 3-week timeline. The gap between them is the case study.

### Result 1 — Proposed Direction Fulfilled the Brief
The original proposed screens covered all 7 flows from the onboarding form — Patient Registry, Patient Catalogue, Program Registration, IV Registration, Schedule Booking, User Profile, and KB Documentation. Each flow was designed to directly address one of the three stated pain points: manual booking, paper records, and no SOP for program assignment.

### Result 2 — Client Direction Reduced Functional Scope
The client replaced the proposed direction with her own designs. Of the 7 original flows, only 3 were retained: Program Registration, IV Registration, and User Profile. Patient Registry, Patient Catalogue, Schedule Booking, and KB Documentation — the flows most directly solving the stated operational problems — were removed entirely. An About Vyepti screen was added, duplicating content already available on vyepti.com.

### Result 3 — Brand Consistency Was Not Maintained in Client Direction
The client's designs also deviated from Vyepti's own brand kit — the same brand kit that was provided to the design team and used as the visual foundation for the proposed direction. The deviation was left in the final file. Accountability for internal brand consistency rests with the client, not the design team.

### Result 4 — Presentation Culture Lesson Learned
The project surfaced a real professional lesson: Singaporean clients expect polished, finished screen presentations before giving approval — not behind-the-scenes or work-in-progress views. This changed how client-facing presentations were approached on all subsequent projects.
*(Observed pattern based on professional experience — not a formal study.)*

## Features

### Patient Registry Form *(Proposed Direction Only)*
A digital intake form replacing Vyepti's paper-based patient registration process. Designed to capture patient information at first contact and feed into the Patient Catalogue — directly addressing the paper records problem stated in the onboarding brief.

### Patient Catalogue Page *(Proposed Direction Only)*
A structured table view of all registered patients — names, treatment status, assigned programs, and related information. The operational hub of the app, giving nurses and secretaries a single source of truth for patient management.

### Schedule Booking Flow *(Proposed Direction Only)*
A calendar-based scheduling interface — referenced from Google Calendar's interaction model — for booking and managing IV treatment appointments. The most direct solution to the manual scheduling problem stated in the brief.

### KB Documentation Page *(Proposed Direction Only)*
An in-app knowledge base providing nurses and secretaries with step-by-step guidance on using the system. Included because the brief identified a lack of SOP as a core pain point — documentation was part of the solution, not an afterthought.

### Program Registration & IV Registration *(Both Directions)*
Two flows retained in both the proposed and client directions — covering treatment program assignment and IV session registration. Present in both versions, though the surrounding flows that gave them full operational context were removed in the client direction.

### Embedded Mini Design System *(Both Directions)*
Every client-facing Figma file produced at Webpuppies included an embedded mini design system page — components, styles, and usage notes — for the developer to reference directly. No separate handoff documentation was needed.

## Research & Discovery

Formal research was not conducted. This was an early-career project and the research limitations are acknowledged honestly.

**Onboarding Form as Primary Brief**
The client's onboarding form was the sole source of functional requirements. All 7 proposed flows were derived directly from what the client stated she needed — no interpretation or assumption was added.

**User Flow Diagrams**
Functional flows were mapped using traditional user flow diagrams, self-conceptualized from the onboarding brief. No interviews with nurses or secretaries were conducted — direct end user access was not part of the engagement, and at this stage of the career, the right questions weren't yet being asked.

**Brand Kit as Visual Foundation**
Vyepti provided a brand kit, which served as the visual reference for the proposed direction. The brand kit left limited room for creative interpretation — which was preferred for a client-facing healthcare project.

**Single Competitor Reference**
Google Calendar was the only external reference used — specifically for the Schedule Booking flow. No broader competitor research into healthcare scheduling tools or appointment booking apps was conducted. This is acknowledged as a gap in hindsight.

---

## Key Insights

**Because the onboarding form was the only brief, it had to be treated as the source of truth.**
Every proposed flow mapped directly to a stated requirement. When the client removed flows in her counter-direction, she was removing solutions to problems she had defined herself. The brief didn't fail — it was overridden.

**Because direct user access wasn't available, the brief carried all the weight.**
Without interviews or observations with nurses and secretaries, the onboarding form was the only signal of what the product needed to do. In hindsight, advocating for even informal user conversations would have grounded the flows in real operational context rather than logical inference.

**Because the client wanted it her way, "Pikit Mata" was the professional call.**
"Pikit Mata" — a Filipino phrase meaning to close your eyes and proceed — describes the practiced skill of executing a client's direction without resistance when escalation isn't an option and the relationship needs to close cleanly. It is not resignation. It is a deliberate, professional choice to prioritize delivery over debate.

**Because Singaporean clients expect finished work in presentations, work-in-progress views undermine trust.**
Showing behind-the-scenes screens — wireframes, flows, explorations — before they're polished creates doubt rather than confidence for this audience. Matching the presentation standard to the client's cultural expectations is as much a design decision as the screens themselves.

## Design Iterations

### Proposed Direction — 7 Flows, Brief-Faithful
The original proposed designs covered the full scope of the onboarding brief: 7 flows, built to Vyepti's brand kit, structured as a logical progression from patient intake to appointment scheduling to in-app documentation. Each flow connected to the next — the Patient Registry fed the Patient Catalogue, the Catalogue informed Program Registration, and the Schedule Booking tied it all together.

### Client Direction — 4 Flows, Client-Defined
After a period of silence — no constructive feedback, no revision requests — the client provided her own screen designs and requested a mobile adaptation. Three of the original 7 flows were retained. Four were removed. An About Vyepti screen was added. The client's designs did not follow Vyepti's brand kit. Ian asked why. The client's response was that she wanted it her way.

The mobile adaptation was completed. The Figma file was delivered. The project closed with a thank-you from the client.

## Reflection

### What Worked
Following the brief exactly — every proposed flow traced directly back to a stated requirement from the onboarding form. The visual direction was grounded in Vyepti's own brand kit rather than personal preference, which gave the proposed designs a defensible, client-aligned foundation. The embedded mini design system ensured Richard had everything he needed without a separate documentation effort.

### What Remained Unresolved
The four removed flows — Patient Registry, Patient Catalogue, Schedule Booking, and KB Documentation — were the functional core of what the app was supposed to solve. Whether the delivered app actually addressed the original operational problems for nurses and secretaries is unknown. No post-launch feedback was received.

### What I'd Do Differently
I'd conduct market validation and competitor research before starting — not to change the flows, but to give them stronger reasoning. At the time, the ignorance was genuine and the brief was followed faithfully. In hindsight, research would have given the proposed direction an evidence base that might have been harder for the client to dismiss.

I'd also present only polished, finished screens to Singaporean clients — never behind-the-scenes work. That lesson has carried into every project since.

On the client direction itself: "Pikit Mata" was the right call. The client was determined, the channel for pushback was limited, and the relationship needed to close professionally. The work was executed cleanly. What the client chose to do with the brief was her responsibility — not a reflection of the design.
