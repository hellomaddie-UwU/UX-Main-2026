## Context

WoofPay was a passion project initiated by the user's boss at Webpuppies, a Singaporean IT company. The product vision was twofold: WoofPay Payments, an affordable alternative to Shopify that helped small business owners with taxes, payments, and a social-media-embeddable online store; and WoofEvents, a form-driven social media post generator designed for entrepreneurs without a marketing team — an early, pre-AI version of what generative tools now do for content creation.

With no existing brand, no prior design system, and a one-month window, a design system needed to be built from scratch while product screens were being designed simultaneously. As the sole UX/UI designer at Webpuppies, the design system wasn't just a deliverable — it was a personal necessity. Without it, maintaining visual consistency across two products and all future marketing outputs would have been unmanageable for one person to sustain.

Richard worked from the design system for two months after handoff without returning with questions or requests. Richard was known to reach out when something was unclear — his continued silence throughout development is interpreted as a signal that the system was coherent and complete enough to build from independently.

No components were requested retroactively. No gaps were identified post-handoff. The scope held exactly as planned, which in a one-month greenfield engagement is a meaningful signal of upfront alignment between design, development, and the product owner.

## Features

### Brand-New Visual Identity System
WoofPay had no existing brand when the project began. Style guide options were presented to the boss upfront — his selection became the locked creative direction for the entire project and all future outputs. This approach prevented mid-project pivots and gave the developer a stable visual reference from day one.

### Cross-Product Component Consistency
All basic components were designed to work across both WoofPay Payments and WoofEvents — ensuring that shared UI patterns (buttons, forms, inputs, navigation) were visually and functionally consistent regardless of which product a user was in.

### Tax Filing Flow (WoofPay Payments)
A custom flow built on top of the basic components, informed by direct research into Xero's tax filing structure and Taxumo's simpler approach. The complexity of Xero's American-oriented tax system informed a deliberate decision to simplify — the WoofPay tax flow was scoped for accessibility and ease of use, not feature parity with enterprise tools.

### Social Media Post Generator Flow (WoofEvents)
A custom Typeform-inspired form flow that outputs a ready-to-edit social media post — the core product experience of WoofEvents. The editing interface was intentionally simplified per the boss's direction: no lasso tools or Photoshop-adjacent features, just a text editor and basic shape controls. The rationale was reducing cognitive load for small business owners who weren't designers.

## Research & Discovery

**Competitor Research — WoofPay Payments**
Maya's web app was studied in full — its structure, purpose, functionality, and interaction patterns. Ecwid was researched as the primary benchmark for affordable Shopify alternatives. A Xero account was created independently to study tax filing workflows firsthand — a self-initiated step beyond the project brief. The Xero deep-dive revealed that American tax filing structures are significantly more complex than the simpler formats used in other markets, which directly informed the decision to simplify WoofPay's Tax Filing flow for a broader, more accessible audience.

**Competitor Research — WoofEvents**
Typeform was studied for its form creation UX and structural approach. Canva's full UI setup and editing experience was mapped to understand what a capable-but-simple editing tool looks and feels like. These two references defined the product's end-to-end experience: guided input on one end, ready-to-use output on the other.

**User Persona**
Both products targeted small business owners and entrepreneurs seeking affordable alternatives to build their business — specifically those who couldn't justify the cost of Shopify, a physical store, or a marketing team. The target audience was designed to be as inclusive and market-agnostic as possible.

**Style Guide Options as Stakeholder Alignment Tool**
Rather than presenting a single direction to the boss, multiple style guide options were developed upfront. The boss's selection locked the visual direction for the entire project — preventing mid-project feedback loops and giving the team a shared reference from the start. This is a consistent personal practice applied across all projects, not a one-off process decision.

## Key Insights

**Because there was no existing brand, the design system and the brand had to be created simultaneously.**
In most design system engagements, a brand already exists and the system codifies it. Here, both had to be invented at the same time. Presenting style options upfront and locking a direction early was essential to avoid the system becoming a moving target.

**Because the developer could extrapolate from clear basics, completeness mattered more than comprehensiveness.**
Richard's request to stop adding components wasn't a limitation — it was feedback that the system was legible. A design system that communicates clearly enough for a developer to continue without the designer present is more valuable than an exhaustive one that still requires constant explanation.

**Because both products shared a user base, the design system had to translate across two very different product experiences.**
WoofPay Payments and WoofEvents serve the same user persona but in entirely different contexts — financial management vs. content creation. The design system needed to feel cohesive across both without flattening the distinct experience each product required.

**Because the project was a one-month engagement, medium fidelity mattered.**
A Figma file served the immediate handoff need. With current tooling and vibe-coding capabilities, an internal component website would have served the developer better — copy-pasting live components is faster than hand-coding from a Figma reference. The constraint was the platform and the timeline, not the approach.

## Design Iterations

### Style Guide Selection as Direction Lock
Early in the project, multiple style guide options were developed and presented to the boss for selection. Once chosen, the direction was treated as locked — no deviations were made throughout the project. This wasn't rigidity; it was a deliberate process for protecting creative credibility and preventing stakeholder-driven drift. Changing an approved direction mid-project signals inconsistency to stakeholders and senior team members.

### WoofEvents Editing Interface Simplified
The initial concept for WoofEvents included a more full-featured editing environment — closer to a design tool with lasso and selection capabilities. The boss requested a significant simplification: remove complex tools, retain only a text editor and basic shape controls. The rationale was explicit — reduce cognitive load for non-designer users, make the tool feel like editing, not designing. This pivot clarified the product's identity and made the interaction model more coherent for its target audience.

### Component Scope Closed Early by Developer
After the basic components were completed, Richard indicated he had enough context to continue independently and didn't need further component documentation. Rather than continuing to build components for their own sake, the scope was closed. Overachieving on a clear instruction is not a virtue in professional work — the right call was to respect the boundary and move on.

## Reflection

### What Worked
Locking the visual direction early through the style guide selection process gave the entire project a stable foundation — no mid-project pivots, no stakeholder-driven rework. Working in parallel with Richard meant the developer had something real to build from almost immediately, rather than waiting for a sequential design-then-develop process. The competitor research — especially the Xero deep-dive — grounded product decisions in real user experience rather than assumptions.

### What Remained Unresolved
The design system was scoped for the two web apps only — marketing outputs (website, emailers, collaterals) were deferred to a future phase. Figma's token/variable system was not yet available at the time of the project; design tokens would have made the system more maintainable and scalable had the platform supported them.

### What I'd Do Differently
I'd build a component website instead of a Figma file — the same conclusion reached on the MSPbot projects. For a developer working in code, a live component library with copy-paste functionality is faster and more practical than navigating a Figma file. The one-month timeline and platform limitations were the real constraints at the time, not the approach itself.

I'd also apply Figma's token and variable system from the start — a feature that didn't exist yet when this project was built, but one that would have made cross-product consistency easier to maintain and update as the brand evolved.
